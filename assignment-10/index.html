First Impressions of McCarthy's All the Pretty Horses

Written by yours truly during junior collage

March 20, 2017

“If this rain hits hard there’ll be a river come down through here like a train. You thought about that? You ain't never been struck by lightnin, said Blevins. You don't know what it’s like. You’ll get drowned sittin there. That’s all right. I ain't never been drowned before.” (Mccarthy 54)

The more I read this book, the more I enjoy it. At first I thought the unconventional style of writing, with the lack of quotations and run on sentences, would be too annoying to read but I have seemed to grow numb to it. I think a general word of advice to anyone about to read this book: try to ignore the incorrect structure and focus on the story.

That being said, the very short lines of dialogue between the characters makes them seem one dimensional. I can gather what role each character plays and a little bit of background of each one, but they still all feel like pieces of cardboard to me.

I noticed that Alejandra was the only character in the story that was described in great detail. This leads me to believe that she will play a huge part in the story not just in being a potential girlfriend to John but in a bigger way too. My prediction is Alejandra will need assistance from John somehow or the other way around.

I think Blevins is a symbol of something. I don't think he’s really meant to be a main character because of how little the two cushions care about him and how he has yet to show back up after they all split. I think that Blevins might be a symbol of the two cousin’s past lives in America. The fact that they have gotten rid of him shows that they have moved on. It makes sense because they both now have jobs and a solid place in their new communities as cowboys. John went as far as claiming that he and Rawlins were the only ones who traveled into Mexico together.

I chose the line of dialog above for an epigraph because it was genuinely funny and made my chuckle as I read it. However, this makes me wonder about Mccarthy’s comic relief placement. The situation the three riders were in seemed like a serious dilemma, but Mccarthy’s use of comic relief made the situation seem like a minor inconvenience on the three’s way into Mexico when in reality, it made Blevins lose everything, even the clothes on his back. I don't know, maybe i’m just over analyzing this one small exchange of words.

I hope to see more character development later in the story. John has a good amount of background to him but his personality comes across as super dull based on his lines of dialogue. I feel like he and the other characters like Rawlins and Alejandra have explosive personalities but have yet to light the fuse.
